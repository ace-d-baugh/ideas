Are you familiar with golden rice?

Yes, I am familiar with golden rice. It is a variety of rice that has been genetically engineered to produce beta-carotene, a precursor to vitamin A. This gives the rice its distinctive golden-yellow color, which is how it got its name.
â€‹The project was developed to address a serious public health problem: vitamin A deficiency (VAD). VAD is a major cause of preventable blindness and can also weaken the immune system, increasing the risk of death from common childhood infections. It is a particular problem in developing countries where rice is a staple food, but the traditional white rice does not contain the necessary micronutrients.
â€‹Scientists, primarily Ingo Potrykus and Peter Beyer, developed golden rice in the 1990s. They introduced genes from daffodils and a common soil bacterium into the rice genome, which enabled the plant to synthesize beta-carotene in its endosperm (the edible part of the grain). A later version, Golden Rice 2, was developed using a gene from corn, which significantly increased the beta-carotene content.
â€‹While it is seen by many as a powerful and cost-effective solution to a widespread health issue, golden rice has also been a source of controversy. Opponents of genetically modified organisms (GMOs) have raised concerns about its safety and potential environmental impacts, despite numerous studies and regulatory approvals that have found it to be as safe as conventional rice. These debates have delayed its widespread adoption, though it has been approved for cultivation in some countries, such as the Philippines.
